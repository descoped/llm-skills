# Naming Strategies

Five strategies for generating domain name candidates. Use all five in each batch, weighted as indicated. The mix is what produces good results — any single strategy alone runs dry quickly.

## Strategy 1: Invented Words (~40% of candidates)

Completely made-up words that are pronounceable, memorable, and mean nothing. This is the hardest strategy and the most valuable — these domains are most likely to be available.

### Phonetic Construction

Build words by combining consonant clusters with vowel sounds:

**Strong openers:** `br`, `cl`, `cr`, `dr`, `fl`, `fr`, `gr`, `kr`, `pl`, `pr`, `qu`, `sc`, `sk`, `sl`, `sn`, `sp`, `st`, `str`, `sw`, `tr`, `tw`, `wr`

**Natural endings:** `-el`, `-on`, `-an`, `-to`, `-ra`, `-vo`, `-nt`, `-th`, `-va`, `-ro`, `-na`, `-lo`, `-ko`

**Two-syllable patterns** (most effective):
- CVC + CVC: `brantol`, `kelvon`, `trovex`
- CCV + CV: `cletho`, `prisna`, `grovya`
- CV + CVC: `jempra`, `fovant`, `kalent`

### Rules

- **Length:** 5-8 characters. Shorter = better. 6 is the sweet spot.
- **Pronounceability:** say it out loud. If you hesitate, skip it.
- **No existing associations:** Google the word. If it means something in any major language, skip it.
- **Avoid saturated endings:** `-ify`, `-ly`, `-io`, `-ai`, `-ex`, `-hub`, `-lab`, `-bit` — these scream "2015 startup."

### Good vs Bad

Good: `cletho`, `brantol`, `jempra`, `trovano`, `holvani`, `krevon`, `dolvari`, `plentho`
Bad: `xqvzth` (unpronounceable), `techify` (saturated ending), `smartly` (real word), `aapio` (vowel soup)

### Successful precedents

Spotify, Figma, Vercel, Prisma, Svelte, Drizzle, Turso, Neon, Supabase, Resend

## Strategy 2: Word Blends / Portmanteau (~20% of candidates)

Combine parts of two relevant words into one new word. The blend should feel natural — if you have to explain the portmanteau, it's too forced.

### Technique

1. Write down 10-15 words related to the project's domain
2. Take the first syllable of one word and the last syllable of another
3. Try multiple combinations until one clicks

### Examples

- `content` + `element` → `contentel` (too long) → `contel`
- `page` + `scaffold` → `pagfold`
- `stream` + `kindle` → `strindle`
- `code` + `frontier` → `cofrontier` (too long) → `codier`

### Rules

- Keep under 10 characters, ideally under 8
- The blend should be pronounceable without knowing the source words
- At least one of the source words should relate to the project's domain

## Strategy 3: Metaphors and Analogies (~15% of candidates)

Words from other domains that metaphorically describe what the project does. These are powerful because they carry instant associations.

### Common metaphor domains

| Project type | Metaphor sources | Examples |
|--------------|-----------------|----------|
| Building/creating | Craftsmanship, construction | `forge`, `anvil`, `kiln`, `loom`, `scaffold` |
| Speed/performance | Nature, physics | `bolt`, `spark`, `pulse`, `flux`, `drift` |
| Security/protection | Military, architecture | `vault`, `bastion`, `sentinel`, `rampart`, `aegis` |
| Data/knowledge | Library, exploration | `atlas`, `lexicon`, `cipher`, `codex`, `archive` |
| Connection/network | Nature, geography | `bridge`, `nexus`, `meridian`, `conduit`, `relay` |
| Growth/scale | Biology, agriculture | `grove`, `canopy`, `bloom`, `harvest`, `root` |

### Technique

Single-word metaphors as `.com` are almost always taken. Combine with a modifier:
- Prefix: `ironkiln`, `redloom`, `darkforge`
- Suffix: `forgekit`, `vaultrun`, `pulsenet`
- Blend: take the metaphor and blend it with a project-relevant word

## Strategy 4: Compressed Phrases / Acronyms (~10% of candidates)

Take a descriptive phrase and compress it into something that sounds like a word.

### Technique

1. Write a 3-5 word description of the project
2. Take initials, first syllables, or key sounds
3. Rearrange until it sounds like a real word

### Examples

- "Structured Content Framework" → `structo`
- "Fast Lightweight Pages" → `flipa`
- "Reliable Event Queue" → `releq`
- "Cloud Native Deploy" → `clonade`

### Rules

- The result must be pronounceable as a word — raw acronyms (`SCFR`) are bad domain names
- Don't force it — if the phrase doesn't compress naturally, try a different phrase

## Strategy 5: Modified Real Words (~15% of candidates)

Take a real word and change 1-2 characters to make it unique while keeping it recognizable.

### Techniques

| Technique | Example | Result |
|-----------|---------|--------|
| Drop vowels | `timber` | `timbr` |
| Swap consonants | `crystal` | `krystal` |
| Change ending | `simple` | `simpel` |
| Add letter | `stack` | `stacko` |
| Swap vowels | `render` | `randir` |
| Double letter | `drop` | `dropp` |
| Language shift | `signal` (EN) | `signel` (Scandinavian feel) |

### Rules

- The original word should be recognizable — the modification is a twist, not a disguise
- Only modify 1-2 characters. More than that and it becomes an invented word (use Strategy 1 instead)
- Check that the modified word doesn't accidentally become an offensive word in another language

## General Anti-Patterns

Avoid these regardless of strategy:

- **Hyphens in domains** — `my-cool-app.com` looks amateur and is hard to say aloud
- **Numbers** — `project42.com` is confusing verbally ("four two or forty-two?")
- **Doubled letters at syllable boundaries** — `mettools` is hard to read
- **Names that require spelling out** — if you'd need to say "that's with a K" every time, reconsider
- **Unintended words** — read the domain as one string: `therapistfinder.com` reads badly
- **Trailing S** — `mytools.com` often gets mistyped as `mytool.com`
